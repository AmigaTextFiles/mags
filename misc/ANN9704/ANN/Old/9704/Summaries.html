68k to PPC ports (30-Apr-97 )

    Dr. Peter Kittel says that Motorola has some tool to convert 68k source into PPC source to offer already, but that This is an approach when you want to see something at all, soon. So this only works for a few, well-written programs, while much hand fiddling and the guy/people who had done the original is required for compilcated programs. The AmigaOS source is said to use dirty tricks like in the 6502 days, like jumping into the middle of another statement to save a few bytes in the executable. Such cases are nightmares for automatic conversion.

ASM vs. C in the Amiga (27-Apr-97 )

    Olaf Barthel gives detailed explanations about what code of the AmigaOS is in C and which is in ASM. He also explains why ASM isn't always the best choice: A project as large as an OS is very complex, and with nothing but ASM you would end up having a complex systems that is hard to maintain, whereas C code is much "cleaner", more organized and easier to verify.

Development at ESCOM and PIOS (10-Apr-97 )

    According to Dave Haynie, the first problem at Escom was that AT didn't hit the ground running with new development. They only contacted Andy and him 7 months after acquiring the Amiga assets. One month later, they showed their PowerAmiga plans to them, but Escom waffled on it and ended up having no money for anything. Some time later at PIOS, development was entirely different. We started knowing we wanted to make a new computer. In summer '96 it looked like a super-low-cost AT box, but as the AmigaOS "disappeared" it became more high-end, starting to look like a BeBox, then evolved to use more current and/or integrated parts and finally was modified once again in mid-January for CHRP.
    Nearly every engineering project is late. The reason is simple -- engineering is both science and art, and there's no way to absolutely predict how quickly inspiration will come when it's needed, how many weird little problems will show up, etc. AT never even got to that stage, though, because they never really started the Power Amiga project. They do have some interesting architecture papers on it...

On AAA (10-Apr-97 )

    Dave Haynie confirms that they (Commodore) had first silicon of the AAA chips; that the chips did stuff, but that they were FAR from complete. With a right budget at the time, he estimates it would have taken 6 to 12 months to finish it. Restarting it now, would mean about two years of work, but the result would be pretty expensive and not very performant. At the time, we were estimating something on the order of $500,000 to $1,000,000 to finish AAA. He thinks that no Amiga chip is a viable option for the desktop today, but for some special purposes ("WebTV" and PCI AmigaToaster card) they could still be suitable. He adds that if he wanted to do something Amiga-like today, he would talk to Ed Ed Hepler and 3DO. On the other side, you wouldn't have had the Amiga without Jay, no matter how much you spent trying to do cool chips.

That person re-wrote it in order to work on the Amiga, and he's implemented some features not in the original, and that are exclusive to that particular port. ^MAX^ is continuing to develop this version, and is actually planning to completely re-write it in assembler. There is now also an official statement from iD software on this subject: we are aware of a port that was recently written for the Amiga. We don't condone, nor do we condemn this version. It is not our intention, at this time, to begin supporting the Amiga, nor in the foreseable future.... But they also say that Amiga users must purchase the original Quake.
